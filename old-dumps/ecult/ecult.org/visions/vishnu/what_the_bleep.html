As for What the Bleep, and so on, I'd say my primary issue with the film is that it was generally very sloppy with how ideas were communicated, and I think in some cases, with the very fabric of those ideas. Examples:

1) Failure to include, or inclusion of ideas _opposite_ to important holistic observations

The presentation of mind / body issues being reduced to neuro-endocrine chemical transmitters was did little in my mind to help undo the current dualist tendencies in both casual and scientific thinking. In this case, I think that "serious" holistic health practices as well as careful science have a much cleaner and clearer understanding of the mind / body problem than was communicated. The cartoon with neurotransmitters or whatever was _way_ long and annoying and (I think) misleading.

How these things should be thought of is in fact a complex issue, and way too involved to discuss fully in an e-mail. Candice Pert, who was actually in the movie, actually has a decent book on that kind of thing (the molecules of emotion). Damasio is also good. Feldenkrais is also good!

In brief, we are a complex of different perceptual, motor and control systems. These systems are not necessarily distinct in the sense that clear lines can be drawn between them. Their structure stems from a variety of genetic / evolved factors, as well as environmental / learned factors. Pleasure, for example, is a useful concept. It is a state which involves a constellation of coordinated sub-states at every level from the balance of peptides in the blood, to the contraction of specific facial muscles leading to a smile. All of these things interact with our consciousness, memory and so on in interesting ways - with modulation occurring in both directions.

2) Inclusion of stuff that just doesn't make sense, and serves to discredit the film to all but the hard-core

The quantum physics thing was just completely not based on anything that is currently believed by a significant number of physicists. I certainly don't know enough to say for sure that you never get a probability distribution of kids on a playground when you turn your back. BUT, most people who keep up with physics in even the most casual sense will immediately dismiss that kind of thing as patent nonsense.

This is a common feature of out-group art, rallies, etc. They will serve as rallying points for a community that feels ignored, but at the same time, they tend to alienate people that might be gained as allies, and provide additional fodder to the powers that be.

One can dismiss the entire movie (or anti-globalization movement, etc.) simply because of a few crazy ideas that seem to ignore large quantities of human knowledge and experience. Just because physicists ignore you doesn't mean you should ignore physicists.
